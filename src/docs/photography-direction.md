# Photography Direction

## Purpose

Photography is the primary storytelling medium of **The Living Threads**.

Rather than functioning as visual decoration, every photograph should deepen the visitor's understanding of Sumba Ikat by revealing the quiet processes, human dedication, and cultural continuity behind each woven textile.

The website is designed as a digital documentary. Photography therefore carries the responsibility of guiding observation, creating emotional rhythm, and reinforcing the editorial narrative established by typography, layout, and motion.

Every image should contribute to the journey rather than simply illustrating information.

---

## Editorial Philosophy

The photographic language follows the conventions of documentary essays, museum publications, and cultural archives.

Images should feel observational, respectful, and timeless.

Photography should never resemble:

- tourism promotion
- commercial advertising
- lifestyle campaigns
- influencer photography
- social media content
- product catalogues
- stock photography

Instead, visitors should feel as though they are quietly witnessing real moments within an ongoing tradition.

The photographs should invite contemplation rather than demand attention.

---

## Visual Narrative

Every image represents only one fragment of a much larger story.

No single photograph should explain everything.

Instead, the complete narrative gradually emerges through the sequence of chapters:

Nature

↓

Craft

↓

Meaning

↓

Living Heritage

↓

Hope

Each chapter should answer the questions raised by the previous one, allowing visitors to slowly discover how nature becomes craftsmanship, craftsmanship becomes meaning, and meaning becomes a living cultural legacy.

Photography should always support this progression.

---

## Human Presence

People exist within the story because culture exists through people.

However, individuals themselves are not the primary subject.

The relationship between people and tradition is.

Whenever possible, photographs should prioritize:

- hands over faces
- gestures over expressions
- observation over interaction
- process over personality

Subjects should never acknowledge the camera.

Avoid eye contact, posing, or performative expressions.

Visitors should feel invisible—as though quietly observing authentic moments rather than participating in them.

---

## Subject Hierarchy

Photography should consistently emphasize creation before completion.

Priority should follow this hierarchy:

1. Hands
2. Traditional tools
3. Natural materials
4. Crafting process
5. Craftspeople
6. Finished textile
7. Environment

Finished textiles should appear only after visitors have witnessed the labour that created them.

The documentary is about the journey of making, not the product itself.

---

## Composition

Composition should communicate restraint and patience.

Preferred characteristics include:

- asymmetrical balance
- generous negative space
- layered depth
- natural framing
- environmental context
- gesture-focused subjects
- visual simplicity

Every composition should feel discovered rather than arranged.

Avoid compositions that appear overly dramatic or intentionally cinematic.

Whitespace within photographs should complement the generous spacing of the website layout.

---

## Framing & Camera Language

The camera should behave like a respectful observer.

Preferred framing includes:

- intimate close-up details
- medium shots of hands and tools
- environmental medium-wide compositions

Wide landscape photography should be used sparingly and only when contributing to the narrative.

Extreme macro photography should remain limited to moments where texture itself carries narrative value.

The overall perspective should resemble quietly walking through a traditional workspace instead of photographing objects for display.

---

## Light & Atmosphere

Natural light is essential.

Preferred lighting conditions include:

- morning daylight
- window light
- open shade
- soft overcast conditions
- diffused natural illumination

Light should gently reveal texture without creating excessive contrast.

Avoid:

- studio lighting
- coloured artificial lighting
- HDR processing
- heavy cinematic grading
- harsh shadows
- dramatic backlighting

Atmosphere should always feel calm, soft, and unhurried.

---

## Colour Palette

Colour should emerge naturally from the environment.

Dominant tones include:

- indigo
- earthy brown
- muted terracotta
- natural white
- weathered wood
- plant green
- stone grey

Neutral colours should dominate the visual experience.

Saturation should remain restrained.

Colour exists to reinforce authenticity rather than visual impact.

---

## Texture & Materiality

Texture communicates craftsmanship.

Photography should celebrate the physical qualities of traditional making, including:

- woven fibres
- rough wood
- natural dye
- weathered tools
- wrinkled hands
- fabric irregularities
- stone surfaces
- dried leaves
- plant materials

Imperfections strengthen authenticity.

They should be preserved rather than removed through editing.

---

## Authenticity

Authenticity takes priority over visual perfection.

Whenever possible, avoid photographs containing:

- tourist performances
- staged demonstrations
- modern branding
- obvious logos
- plastic containers
- smartphones
- synthetic decorations
- commercial packaging
- heavily modern interiors

Traditional environments should remain visually dominant.

The documentary should celebrate living culture rather than idealized nostalgia.

---

## Emotional Progression

Each chapter carries its own emotional atmosphere while remaining visually connected to the others.

### Hero

Wonder.

Photography introduces curiosity without revealing too much.

Images should feel poetic and inviting.

---

### Nature

Observation.

Visitors begin discovering where every thread originates.

Images should feel quiet, organic, and patient.

---

### Craft

Energy.

The visual rhythm becomes more active through gestures, hands, and repeated manual actions.

Energy should emerge from movement rather than dramatic composition.

---

### Meaning

Reflection.

Photography becomes slower and more contemplative.

Subjects should communicate symbolism, memory, and identity.

---

### Living Heritage

Continuity.

Images reveal relationships between generations and everyday cultural life.

The focus shifts from individual actions to collective preservation.

---

### Hope

Release.

Photography becomes increasingly minimal.

Subjects appear lighter, calmer, and more spacious.

The documentary concludes by quietly fading rather than ending.

---

## Continuity Across Chapters

Although each chapter carries a different emotional tone, every photograph should appear as though it belongs to the same documentary project.

Consistency should be maintained across:

- lighting
- colour temperature
- contrast
- camera perspective
- editing style
- visual rhythm
- emotional restraint

Visitors should never feel that photographs originate from unrelated photographers or separate collections.

---

## Image Quality Standards

Authenticity is more important than technical perfection.

Preferred characteristics include:

- high resolution
- natural grain
- realistic colour
- preserved highlight detail
- preserved shadow detail
- moderate contrast

Avoid:

- excessive sharpening
- artificial clarity
- aggressive colour grading
- oversaturated colours
- unrealistic dynamic range

Images should remain believable.

---

## Success Criteria

The photography direction is considered successful when visitors no longer perceive individual photographs but instead experience a single continuous documentary.

Every image should answer at least one of these questions:

- Where does this tradition begin?
- Who keeps this knowledge alive?
- How is craftsmanship passed from one generation to another?
- What labour exists behind beauty?
- Why does this tradition continue to matter today?

If a photograph is merely beautiful but does not strengthen the narrative, it does not belong in the collection.
