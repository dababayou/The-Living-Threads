# Nature Layout Brief

Status: 🔒 Blueprint

This document translates the Nature Story Freeze into spatial, structural, and editorial design decisions. It is the definitive guide for layout implementation in Sprint 3B. No code or implementation details are discussed—only how the narrative manifests in space.

---

## 1. Spatial Mission
To ground the user. The space must transition the experience from the airy, abstract heights of the Hero section down into the dense, tactile, and rooted reality of the natural world.

## 2. Spatial Emotion
**Intimate and Expansive.** The layout must balance vast, breathable environmental context with hyper-focused, macro-level intimacy. It should feel like stepping into a quiet forest: vast overhead, but immediately detailed at your fingertips.

## 3. Vertical Rhythm
**The Descent.** The vertical rhythm relies on a deliberate slowing of pace. 
- **Top:** Wide, sweeping margins (Atmosphere).
- **Middle:** Closer cropping, asymmetrical text placement (Discovery).
- **Bottom:** Tight, grounded, and rich intersections of image and text (Extraction).
Scrolling should feel like digging gently into the earth.

## 4. Section Density
The density increases progressively. It begins sparse (matching the Hero's breathing room) and gradually condenses. Images should overlap slightly, and typography should move closer to the visual elements, creating a woven, intertwined composition rather than isolated blocks.

## 5. Visual/Text Ratio
**80% Visual / 20% Text.** 
Observation precedes explanation. Large visual canvases must dominate the viewport, forcing the user to feel the atmosphere before their eyes naturally discover the supporting typography.

## 6. Reading Width
**Narrow and Intimate.** While imagery may stretch full-bleed or span wide grids, the typography must remain tightly constrained. This forces a focused, intimate reading experience amid the vast natural visuals.

## 7. Eye Path
**Meandering.** The eye should not drop in a straight vertical line. The layout must use asymmetry to guide the user's gaze in a gentle "S" or "Z" curve, mimicking a winding path through a landscape or the natural curve of a root.

## 8. Negative Space Strategy
Negative space here is not "empty"—it is "atmosphere." The background should feel like air or soil. Margins between images should be generous enough to prevent clutter but intentional enough to show the relationship between elements.

## 9. Composition Principles
- **No Floating Artifacts:** Elements must feel grounded. Use overlapping frames or anchor elements to the grid's edges.
- **Asymmetry is Natural:** Avoid perfect, mirrored symmetry. Nature is balanced but irregular.
- **Scale Contrast:** Pair massive, expansive textures with very small, delicate typographic moments.

## 10. Scroll Illusion Strategy
The layout should use z-depth and overlap to create friction. As the user scrolls, background textures should linger while foreground details (leaves, fibers) overlap them, creating a sensation of walking through an environment rather than scrolling a flat page.

## 11. Transition from Hero
**The Settling Dust.** The transition must not use a harsh horizontal line or sudden background color shift. Instead, the Hero's spaciousness should naturally bleed into the top of the Nature section, where a large, soft environmental texture gradually emerges from the bottom up, catching the user like soft ground.

## 12. Transition into Craft
**The Handoff.** The section must end densely, focusing on the raw extracted materials (thread, dye). The final spatial block should align perfectly center, creating a structured, focused focal point that prepares the eye for the rhythmic, human-made symmetry of the upcoming Craft section.

## 13. Visual Tempo
**Largo (Slow and broad).** Large blocks of content requiring long scrolls. The distance between major narrative beats should be substantial, preventing the user from consuming the information too quickly.

## 14. Editorial Principles
- **Show, Don't Tell:** If a texture can explain the roughness of bark, remove the adjective from the text.
- **Captions as Poetry:** Use small, secondary text styles for micro-observations attached to imagery, treating them like field notes.
- **Respect the Edge:** Allow textures and imagery to bleed off the edges of the screen, implying a world larger than the viewport.

## 15. Human Review Checklist
Before moving to implementation, the layout must pass these questions:
- [ ] Does the eye wander naturally instead of reading straight down?
- [ ] Do the visual elements feel like one connected ecosystem rather than a gallery?
- [ ] Is the text secondary to the visual atmosphere?
- [ ] Does the pace feel slower and more deliberate than the Hero?
- [ ] Is the transition from the Hero seamless and grounded?
